Lecture 01

Morphology of Culture. Language of Culture

The foundation of everything: What is culture, how is it structured, and how does it communicate?

Historical Development Morphology Language of Culture Definitions of Culture

A. Lecture Overview

This foundational lecture traces the historical evolution of the concept of culture from its Latin agricultural roots to its modern interdisciplinary usage. It introduces the morphology of culture — the internal structural architecture that makes culture a coherent system — and positions culture as a form of language through which society communicates values, meanings, and identity.

Culture is not a fixed object. It is a dynamic, multi-phase process shaped by human adaptation, labor, and symbolic creation. By the end of this lecture, you should understand culture as simultaneously a social institution, a normative system, a symbolic framework, and a tool for human survival.

B. Core Concepts & Definitions

Etymology of “Culture”

From Latin colere — to cultivate, to inhabit, to honor. As a noun: act or result of cultivating. Earliest use required a qualifier: cultura animi (cultivation of the soul). The term agri-cultura (agriculture) meant the cultivation of the land.

Cicero’s Cultura Animi

Roman philosopher Cicero, in Tusculanae Disputationes (1st century BCE), used the agricultural metaphor to describe philosophical mind cultivation — the development of the soul as the highest human goal. This is the first recorded metaphorical extension of the word “culture.”

Edward Tylor’s Definition (1871)

“Culture or civilization, taken in its wide ethnographic sense, is that complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom, and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society.” — Primitive Culture (1871). The first scientific definition of culture in anthropology.

Cultural Studies as a Discipline

The term cultural studies (kulturologiya) was introduced by American anthropologist Leslie Alvin White, founder of neo-evolutionism in cultural anthropology. White defined culture as the class of things and events dependent upon symboling, establishing it as a proper scientific field.

Morphology of Culture

A branch of cultural studies that analyzes the structure of culture as a social phenomenon — focusing on the regularities of its composition and the processes by which forms of cultural phenomena are created. Morphology views culture as a system composed of structural elements that can be classified, compared, and analyzed.

Material Culture

Tangible, physical objects created and used by humans: tools, buildings, clothing, food, infrastructure. Material culture reflects and embodies the values and functions of the society that produced it. Artifacts are at its core — objects transformed by human intention.

Spiritual (Non-Material) Culture

Intangible cultural products: beliefs, values, norms, art, philosophy, religion, rituals, oral traditions, law. In early civilizations, material and spiritual culture were unified in a state known as syncretism — no distinction between physical and metaphysical realms.

Syncretism

The unity of the physical and metaphysical realms in primitive societies. Early humans did not distinguish themselves from their natural environment, producing totemism (identification with animals) and animism (attribution of human qualities to inanimate objects).

Meme (Dawkins, 1976)

Introduced by Richard Dawkins in The Selfish Gene. A meme is a unit of cultural transmission or imitation — the cultural equivalent of a biological gene. Memes spread, mutate, and are selected through culture. Stages: meme → theme → dream (ritual).

Three Elements of Culture

Values: ideas about what in life seems important. Norms: expectations of how people will behave in different situations. Artifacts: material objects derived from the culture’s values and norms. (After Kroeber & Kluckhohn)

Culture vs. Civilization

Culture: internal character of society — values, beliefs, ethics, traditions, religion, morals, norms, social behavior, worldview. Civilization: external manifestation — tools, cities, technology, infrastructure, agriculture, social institutions, physical/material development. They are complementary, not identical.

Kroeber & Kluckhohn’s 8 Categories (1952)

In Culture: A Critical Review (1952), they compiled 200+ definitions organized into 8 categories: Topical, Historical, Behavioral, Normative, Functional, Mental, Structural, Symbolic. These categories remain a cornerstone of cultural studies methodology.

C. Expanded Academic Notes

Historical Phases of the Concept of Culture

Antiquity: Culture as action directed toward something (cultivation of land, soul, mind). Used only with qualifiers.

Middle Ages: The word “culture” receded; “cult” took precedence (cult of God, cult of rituals, cult of chivalry). Cultural development continued through religious frameworks.

Renaissance: Culture associated with personal development, humanistic ideals, the well-rounded individual. Masterpieces like Michelangelo’s David embodied this ideal.

Enlightenment (17th–18th c.): Montesquieu, Voltaire, Herder — culture expressed through reason, social order, and political institutions. The “cult of reason” became synonymous with culture. Progress = movement from barbarism to enlightened state.

19th century: Culture formalized as an analytical category. Begins to intersect with “civilization.” Term enters scientific discourse.

20th century (Marxist view): Culture connected to material production and social relations. Each form of culture reflects human labor and transformations of nature and society.

The Symbolic View of Culture

Associated with Clifford Geertz and Victor Turner. Symbols are both the practices of social actors and the context that gives such practices meaning. Anthony Cohen described “Symbolic Laws” — common symbols allow communication while still carrying personal significance. Symbols make culture possible, reproducible, and readable. Geertz’s famous phrase: culture as “webs of significance.”

Sociobiological Theory & Meme Progression

Dawkins’ meme theory has a developmental hierarchy:

  1. Meme: basic unit of cultural transmission (e.g., Kazakh hospitality — “when a guest arrives, blessings come with them”)
  2. Theme: conscious behavioral response based on internalized memes (e.g., serving tea and food warmly to visitors)
  3. Dream/Ritual: deeply embedded worldview behavior (e.g., hosting rituals at Nauryz, weddings, funerals with extensive meals)

Sociobiologists define culture as “a method of producing memes.” Meme transmission is essential for cultural survival.

Culture as a Superbiological Mechanism

Despite the diversity of definitions, culture can be understood as:

  • A superbiological means of adaptation (how communities adjust to changing environments)
  • Forms and methods of human communication
  • Humanity’s collective memory
  • A normative and inherited system for programming social behavior
  • A characteristic of a particular type or stage of societal development
  • A unity of human actions, relationships, and institutions supporting social stability

D. Key Thinkers & Scholars

Edward Burnett Tylor

1832–1917 • British Anthropology

First scientific definition of culture (1871). Defined culture as a “complex whole” including all capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society. Founded modern cultural anthropology.

Theory: Unilinear cultural evolutionism — all cultures progress through the same stages (savagery → barbarism → civilization).

Leslie Alvin White

1900–1975 • American Anthropology

Introduced the term “kulturologiya” (cultural studies) as a distinct scientific discipline. Founded neo-evolutionism in cultural anthropology.

Theory: Culture defined as the class of things and events dependent upon symboling. Energy capture as the engine of cultural evolution.

Clifford Geertz

1926–2006 • American Anthropology

Symbolic/interpretive anthropology. Defined culture as “webs of significance” that man himself has spun. Advocated “thick description” as the method of cultural analysis.

Theory: Culture is not a power but a context within which social events, behaviors, and processes can be intelligibly described.

Richard Dawkins

1941– • British Evolutionary Biology

Introduced the concept of the meme in The Selfish Gene (1976). The meme as the cultural analog of the gene — a unit of cultural transmission and replication.

Theory: Cultural evolution operates by selection of memes, analogous to natural selection of genes. Memes that are more memorable, useful, or appealing spread more widely.

Kroeber & Kluckhohn

1876–1960 / 1905–1960 • American Anthropology

Compiled 200+ definitions of culture (1952). Organized them into 8 analytical categories, providing the most comprehensive taxonomy of cultural definitions in the history of the discipline.

Theory: No single definition suffices; the diversity of definitions reflects the multidimensionality of culture itself.

F. Real-World Examples

🇰🇿 Kazakh Hospitality as a Meme System

The Kazakh saying “Qonaq keldi — bata keldi” (when a guest arrives, blessings come) is a cultural meme. It becomes a theme in the practice of serving tea and food, and a ritual/dream in the elaborate hosting ceremonies at Nauryz, weddings, and funerals with extensive dastarkhan spreads.

🌎 Renaissance as Cultural Moment

Michelangelo’s David and Raphael’s Sistine Madonna embodied the Renaissance ideal of the well-rounded, intellectually and morally developed human being — culture as personal perfection.

🇰🇿 Material vs. Spiritual Culture in Kazakh Context

The yurt is simultaneously material culture (felt walls, wooden framework, portable structure) and spiritual culture (cosmic symbolism — the circular hole [tunduk] representing heaven, the central hearth representing life). This dual nature illustrates how material and spiritual culture interpenetrate.

K. Model Answers ✏

Culture vs. Civilization (Model Answer)

Culture and civilization are two complementary but distinct dimensions of society. Culture refers to the internal character of society — the values, beliefs, ethics, morals, traditions, religion, and norms that bind a community together. It is what people believe, value, and practice. Civilization, by contrast, refers to the external manifestation — the tools, cities, infrastructure, technology, agriculture, and institutions that a society builds. It is what people build, organize, and leave behind.

For example, in Kazakh culture: Islamic values and hospitality norms (culture) coexist with the architecture of Turkestan city and the Mausoleum of Khoja Ahmed Yasawi (civilization). Both are necessary for a complete picture of Kazakh society.

Dawkins’ Meme Theory with Kazakh Example (Model Answer)

Richard Dawkins introduced the meme in The Selfish Gene (1976) as the cultural equivalent of the biological gene — a unit of cultural transmission that spreads through imitation and replication. Cultural acquisition proceeds through three stages:

  1. Meme: The basic cultural unit (e.g., the Kazakh saying “when a guest arrives, blessings come”)
  2. Theme: Conscious behavioral response based on internalized memes (e.g., always serving tea and food warmly to any guest)
  3. Dream/Ritual: Deeply embedded worldview behavior (e.g., the elaborate hosting ceremonies at Nauryz, weddings, and funerals, where hospitality is treated as sacred)

This progression shows how a simple cultural idea becomes embedded as a lived worldview through repetition and social reinforcement.

Lecture 02

Semiotics of Culture. Anatomy of Culture

How culture encodes, transmits, and decodes meaning through signs, symbols, and texts

Semiotics Signs & Symbols Saussure Peirce Lotman Barthes

A. Lecture Overview

Semiotics is the study of signs and sign systems. Applied to culture, it reveals how society creates, transmits, and interprets meaning. This lecture examines two major traditions in semiotics (European/Saussurean and American/Peircean), the Tartu-Moscow School of Cultural Semiotics (Lotman), and key extensions by Barthes, Kristeva, Morris, and Vygotsky.

Culture, from a semiotic perspective, functions as a generator of structure — a vast system of codes that shapes how people behave, communicate, think, and construct identity.

B. Core Concepts & Definitions

Sign (General Definition)

Anything that stands for something else in the process of communication or thought. Signs are the fundamental building blocks of meaning-making in culture.

Saussure’s Dyadic Sign Model

Ferdinand de Saussure divided the sign into: Signifier (the sound-image or form — the word or image itself) and Signified (the concept or mental idea it represents). Meaning is relational — arising from differences within a system, not from inherent properties. Key principle: the connection between signifier and signified is arbitrary.

Synchrony vs. Diachrony (Saussure)

Synchrony: studying a language/culture system at a single moment in time. Diachrony: studying it across time, tracking historical change. Saussure prioritized synchronic analysis, arguing meaning is created within a system at a given moment.

Peirce’s Triadic Sign Model

Charles Sanders Peirce distinguished three types of signs based on their relationship to what they represent: Icon (resembles its object: a photograph, map, pictogram), Index (has a logical/causal connection: smoke = fire, footprint = person), Symbol (arbitrary convention: national flag, national anthem).

Interpretant (Peirce)

The mental concept or understanding created by a sign in a person’s mind. Meaning is not a direct link between sign and object but a mental process in the interpretive mind. This process is called semiosis — iterative and potentially infinite (unlimited semiosis).

Semiotics (Greek: semeion)

The study of signs and sign systems. From Greek semeion = sign. Roots in ancient Greek Stoic philosophy. The modern concept of cultural semiotics was developed by Yuri Lotman in the 20th century.

Lotman’s Semiosphere

Yuri Lotman (Tartu-Moscow School) defined culture as “a unified whole made up of inherited information that society accumulates, preserves, and transmits across generations.” The semiosphere is the total sign-space of a culture. All cultural texts (novels, rituals, monuments, fashion) are governed by invisible codes.

Text (Semiotic Definition)

In Lotman’s semiotics, a “text” is any cultural artifact carrying meaning — not just written language, but also a religious ritual, a song, a monument, a fashion trend, a national flag. All texts are governed by cultural codes.

Myth (Barthes)

Roland Barthes defined myth as a second-order system of signs. A sign (signifier + signified) takes on an additional ideological layer of meaning when it becomes culturally entrenched. Myth naturalizes ideology — making historical and class-based meanings appear universal and inevitable.

Morris’s Three Subfields of Semiotics

Semantics: relationship between signs and what they represent. Syntactics: relationships between signs (rules of combination, like grammar). Pragmatics: effect of signs on human behavior and social interaction.

Sémanalyse (Kristeva)

Julia Kristeva’s approach to semiotics. Proposes that the study of meaning must go beyond stable sign units to examine the historical development and dynamic transformation of meaning within cultural and social contexts. History itself can be read as a system of signs.

Inner Speech (Vygotsky)

Lev Vygotsky applied semiotic thinking to developmental psychology. Language is a cognitive and cultural tool. Higher-order thinking (reasoning, problem-solving) occurs through inner speech — internalized language that guides behavior from within. Children first verbalize plans externally, then internalize them.

C. Expanded Academic Notes

Saussure vs. Peirce: Two Traditions in Semiotics

European tradition (Saussure): Focused on language as a structured system. Signs are dyadic (signifier + signified). Meaning is relational and systemic. Structuralism follows from this model — meaning emerges from differences within a system, not from individual elements in isolation. Led to structuralism in linguistics, anthropology, and literary theory.

American tradition (Peirce): Broader view encompassing logic, thought, perception. Signs are triadic (sign + object + interpretant). Semiosis is an ongoing chain of interpretations. Peirce was interested not just in language but in all forms of meaning-making — actions, habits, gestures, anything that “stands for something else.”

Together: Saussure shows us how meaning is structured in language; Peirce shows how meaning is created in the mind and evolves through interpretation.

Barthes’ Myth & Ideology in Culture

In Mythologies (1963), Barthes analyzed everyday French culture (wrestling, wine, steak and chips, the Eiffel Tower) to reveal hidden ideological messages. A soldier saluting a flag appears to signify patriotism, but Barthes reveals it also communicates a myth: that loyalty, national pride, and obedience to authority are natural, inevitable, and desirable.

Barthes introduced the “structural man” — someone who engages the world by uncovering the hidden frameworks governing systems, whether in literature, fashion, or mass media.

Kazakhstan connection: Kazakhstani national symbols (the golden eagle, the Bayterek tower) function as Barthesian myths — carrying ideological messages about national strength, sovereignty, and modernity beyond their immediate visual meaning.

Olzhas Suleimenov & Semiotic Nationalism

Olzhas Suleimenov (born in Almaty, 1936) is a Kazakh poet and essayist who applied semiotic methods to reinterpret Slavic and Turkic cultural history. His groundbreaking book Az i Ya (1975) analyzed the medieval Russian epic The Song of Igor’s Campaign to reveal hidden Turkic linguistic layers.

His title Az i Ya is itself a semiotic act: splitting the Russian word for “Asia” into syllables that spell “I and I” — symbolizing a hybrid Slavic-Turkic identity. The book argued that many parts of world history, especially those concerning literate peoples, cannot be understood without knowledge of Turkic history. This made it politically controversial in the Soviet Union and created a sensation among Kazakh intellectuals.

Digital Nomadism as Semiotic Phenomenon

The modern concept of digital nomadism carries powerful semiotic dimensions. The term recycles the ancient Kazakh nomadic identity (freedom, movement, adaptability) and applies it to contemporary information-era work practices. Irina Kushilevа-Sagan describes digital nomadism as “a social-cultural phenomenon conceptually belonging to modern information and communication technologies.”

The motto “freedom without limitation” functions as a cultural myth (in Barthes’ sense): it naturalizes a set of power relations (freelance precarity, dependency on internet infrastructure) by presenting them as liberation.

D. Key Thinkers & Scholars

Ferdinand de Saussure

1857–1913 • Swiss Linguistics

Father of structural linguistics. Proposed the dyadic sign model (signifier/signified), the arbitrary nature of the sign, and the priority of synchronic analysis. Founded the European tradition of semiotics.

Key works: Course in General Linguistics (published posthumously, 1916). Key concepts: langue/parole, synchrony/diachrony, syntagm/paradigm.

Charles Sanders Peirce

1839–1914 • American Philosophy

Founder of American semiotics. Triadic sign model (sign/object/interpretant), three sign types (icon/index/symbol), and the concept of unlimited semiosis.

Pragmatism: meaning is determined by the practical effects of an idea. Semiotics as a universal logic of signs encompassing all human communication and thought.

Yuri Lotman

1922–1993 • Tartu-Moscow School

Founder of cultural semiotics. Developed the concept of the semiosphere. Defined culture as a system of inherited information governed by codes. Extended semiotic analysis to all cultural texts.

Key works: Universe of the Mind, Structure of the Artistic Text. The semiosphere as the total sign-space within which all human communication takes place.

Roland Barthes

1915–1980 • French Literary Theory

Mythologies & second-order semiotics. Extended Saussurean semiotics to popular culture, fashion, photography, and ideology. Defined myth as naturalized ideology.

Key works: Mythologies (1957), S/Z, Camera Lucida. “The death of the author” — meaning is made by readers, not fixed by authors.

Julia Kristeva

1941– • French-Bulgarian Philosophy & Feminism

Introduced sémanalyse — a critique of how meaning is produced in texts. Challenged the stability of Saussurean signs. Introduced the concept of intertextuality: all texts are made of references to other texts.

Key works: Séméiôtiké (Semiotics, 1969). The semiotic/symbolic distinction: pre-linguistic drives (semiotic) vs. structured language (symbolic).

Charles Morris

1901–1979 • American Philosophy

Behaviorist semiotics. Built on Peirce’s model but introduced a behavioral dimension. Signs as goal-directed tools. Divided semiotics into semantics, syntactics, and pragmatics.

Signs are not just passive symbols but actively guide and control human actions. Four components of semiosis: sign vehicle, interpreter, designatum, interpretant.

Lev Vygotsky

1896–1934 • Soviet Psychology

Semiotics applied to cognitive development. Language as a cultural tool that shapes thought. Inner speech as the internalization of external linguistic tools. Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD).

Key work: Mind in Society. Higher mental functions originate in social interaction. Thought and language, initially separate, merge in development.

Olzhas Suleimenov

1936– • Kazakh Poet & Cultural Scholar

Semiotic reinterpretation of Eurasian cultural history. In Az i Ya (1975), he decoded hidden Turkic layers in Slavic medieval texts, arguing for the deep Turkic roots of Eurasian civilization.

Hybrid identity: the “I and I” of Az i Ya represents the Slavic-Turkic composite self. Critical of Eurocentric historiography; advocate for internationalist historical consciousness rooted in native history.

A sign with a causal/logical connection to its object. Example: smoke is an index of fire; a footprint is an index of a person who walked there.

What is the “semiosphere”?

Yuri Lotman’s concept: the total sign-space of a culture — the entire semiotic environment within which all human communication takes place. Culture generates structure and meaning within this space.

What is Barthes’ concept of “myth”?

A second-order sign system in which an existing sign (with its own signifier and signified) becomes the signifier for a new, ideological signified. Myth naturalizes ideology, making historical constructs appear natural and inevitable.

Lecture 03

Nomads’ Culture of Kazakhstan. The Cultural Heritage of the Proto-Turkic People

From the ancient steppe: Tengriism, Saka singers, nomadic worldview, and the roots of Kazakh identity

Nomadism Tengriism Proto-Turkic Scythians/Sakas Ancient Turks Mythology

A. Lecture Overview

This lecture explores the cultural world of the proto-Turkic and ancient Turkic peoples who inhabited the Eurasian steppe — the predecessors of the Kazakh nation. It covers the nomadic worldview, the cosmogonic system of Tengriism (the worship of the sky deity Tengri), the mythology of the ancient Turks, and the remarkable Scythian-Saka sages who bridged steppe and Mediterranean civilizations.

Nomadism is not a primitive or undeveloped mode of life — it is a sophisticated system of ecological adaptation, social organization, spiritual life, and cultural production that created rich oral traditions, complex belief systems, and distinctive material culture.

B. Core Concepts & Definitions

Nomadism

A mode of life based on seasonal migration following pasture and water sources. For Kazakh ancestors, nomadism was not primitive wandering but a sophisticated system of ecological adaptation: seasonal migrations (winter quarters/qystau, summer pastures/zhailan), livestock herding, and structured social organization around the aul (mobile community). It formed the basis of identity, ritual, and oral tradition.

Tengriism

The ancient belief system of the Turkic and Mongolic peoples centered on Tengri (the sky deity). Tengri and the sky were synonymously used. It has its own hierarchy of deities: Buddh Tengri (supreme deity, symbol of prosperity), Jol Tengri (God of the Way — freedom and boldness), Viruth Tengri (takes souls of dead warriors to the sky), and Erlik (master of the underworld). Tengriism coexisted with and absorbed elements of Zoroastrianism, Buddhism, and eventually Islam.

Syncretism in Ancient Turkic Culture

Ancient Turkic mythology was characterized by syncretism — the blending of mythological, religious, social, and cultural activities without clear distinctions between them. There were no separate terms for dance, religion, cult, or ritual. All activities were part of an undivided cosmic unity. This parallels the syncretism discussed in Lecture 1.

Cosmogony

A theory or myth of the origin of the universe — explaining the structure and order of the world in terms of divine or mythological creation (Merriam-Webster). In Kazakh cultural context, Tengriism provided a cosmogonic framework showing how ancient beliefs shaped cosmogony, ritual, and perception of human place in the cosmos.

Totemism & Animism

Totemism: identification of a social group with a specific animal or natural object, which functions as a spiritual ancestor or protector. Animism: attribution of souls, spirits, or human qualities to animals, plants, and inanimate objects. Both emerged from the syncretic unity of humans with their natural environment.

Saka Sages: Toxaris & Anacharsis

Two remarkable Scythian-Saka intellectuals who achieved renown in ancient Athens: Toxaris — a Scythian healer who became revered in Athens as a physician; after his death during the Athens Plague, his ghost advised citizens to water streets with wine, reducing illness. Anacharsis — a Scythian philosopher who was a member of the Athenian Areopagus (highest governing body), participated in the Olympic Games, and was cited by Herodotus, Aristotle, Plutarch, and Montaigne. His philosophy emphasized ethics: “Speech is bad when thoughts are bad; speech cannot be bad if thoughts are good and good action follows.”

Cultural Ecology

Julian Steward’s concept that culture is not isolated from geography or climate but is a dynamic system of adaptation between human communities and their environment. Perfectly illustrated by the Kazakh nomadic lifestyle: seasonal migrations, livestock herding, and pasture use were not just survival strategies but the foundation of identity, ritual, and oral tradition.

Proto-Turkic Peoples

The ancestral Turkic-speaking populations who inhabited the Eurasian steppe before the formation of distinct Turkic khanates. Includes the Saka/Scythians, Massagetae, Wusun (Usun), Kangly, and Huns — all contributing to the cultural DNA of later Kazakh civilization.

C. Expanded Academic Notes

The Religious Trajectory of the Ancient Turks

Ancient Turkic religion was not monolithic but evolved through several phases:

  1. Indigenous animism/totemism: Veneration of nature, ancestors, fire, water, sacred animals (especially horse and eagle)
  2. Tengriism: Organized sky-worship with a hierarchy of deities; parallel to but distinct from Zoroastrianism
  3. Contact with world religions: 8th–9th centuries: exposure to Manichaeism, Buddhism, Nestorianism in the Altai and East Turkestan
  4. Islamization: The Karakhanid Khanate proclaimed Islam as the state religion in the mid-10th century; by the end of the century, Islamization of the Eurasian steppe was largely complete
  5. Syncretic Islam: Tengriism elements persisted within Islamic practice (veneration of ancestors, sacred sites, fire rituals, concepts like “Allah-Tangry berdi” — combining Islamic Allah with indigenous Tengri)
Tengri Hierarchy of Deities

According to Russian scholar Ajigalov, the Tengriist hierarchy includes:

  • Buddh Tengri: The supreme deity at the center of the Turkic world; symbol of happy life and prosperity; connected to the divine right of rulers and formation of the Turkic state
  • Jol Tengri (God of the Way): Symbol of daring and freedom; messenger of Tengri who transmits the heavenly code to the most worthy and brave people
  • Viruth Tengri: Takes the souls of dead warriors to the sky
  • Erlik: Master of the underworld

Additionally, Turkic peoples worshipped: the cult of fire, veneration of ancestors, respect for tombs, belief in spirits through water. These beliefs persist within Kazakh Islamic practice today.

Anacharsis: The Scythian Philosopher in Athens

Anacharsis is one of the most remarkable figures connecting Central Asian steppe civilization with the ancient Greek world. A Scythian by origin, he traveled to Athens and:

  • Became a member of the Areopagus — the highest governing body of Athens
  • Met Solon, the great Athenian lawmaker and philosopher
  • Participated in the Olympic Games
  • Was cited by Herodotus (Histories), Aristotle (Nicomachean Ethics), Plutarch, Lucian of Samosata, Montaigne, and Diogenes

His famous ethical principle: “Speech is bad when thoughts are bad. Speech cannot be bad if thoughts are good and good action follows.” This philosophy shows that ethical sophistication and moral philosophy were not absent from the steppe world — they simply used different forms of expression.

Lecture 04

Medieval Culture of Central Asia. The Cultural Heritage of the Turks

Urban civilization, intellectual giants, and the spiritual power of Sufism on the Silk Road

Medieval Cities Karakhanids Al-Farabi Al-Kashgari Yusuf Balasaguni Sufism Silk Road

A. Lecture Overview

Medieval Central Asia (9th–15th centuries) was one of the most vibrant intellectual and cultural zones in the world. The cities of southern Kazakhstan — Otrar, Turkistan (Shavgar/Yasy), Taraz, Isfijab, Suyab — were key nodes on the Great Silk Road, hosting caravans, scholars, and artisans from across Eurasia. This lecture covers three major themes:

  1. The rise of urban culture under the Karakhanid Khanate — city structure, bazaars, craft guilds, trade
  2. The intellectual legacy of Al-Kashgari (Turkic lexicography) and Al-Farabi (philosophy, music, political theory), plus Yusuf Balasaguni’s ethical treatise
  3. Sufism as a powerful religious and cultural movement that shaped Central Asia before and after the Mongol invasion, including its unique Kazakh expression and the legacy of Khoja Ahmed Yasawi

B. Core Concepts & Definitions

Medieval Urban Structure (Three Parts)

Central Asian medieval cities had three zones: Citadel (Ark) — the most fortified central part; housed palaces of rulers decorated with paintings, clay carvings, and carved wood. Shahristan — the central city nucleus; residence of the ruler and nobility, including the citadel and walls. Rabat — suburban craft settlement located outside the shahristan; where artisans, traders, and ordinary citizens lived.

Karakhanid Khanate

A Turkic dynasty (9th–12th centuries) that controlled South-Eastern Central Asia, including parts of present-day Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, and Kyrgyzstan. First Turkic state to adopt Islam as the state religion (mid-10th century). Produced major intellectual figures including Yusuf Balasaguni and Mahmud al-Kashgari.

Kutadgu Bilig (Yusuf Balasaguni, 1069–70)

The first major philosophical-literary work in the Turkic language. The title translates as “Blessed Knowledge” or “Wisdom That Brings Happiness.” A political treatise and ethical guide setting out norms of the ideal society, behavioral rules for different social classes, and principles of righteous governance: observance of law, intelligence, and the happiness of righteous life.

Mahmud al-Kashgari’s Diwan Lughat al-Turk

The most comprehensive medieval dictionary of Turkic languages, written in the 11th century. An invaluable and unique source on medieval Turkic life, describing: material culture (clothing, dwellings, tools, weapons), food and drink, animal husbandry, geographical names, astronomical terms, medical vocabulary, kinship terms, historical and mythological heroes, religious vocabulary, and children’s games.

Al-Farabi (870–950)

Abu Nasr Muhammad Ibn Tarhan Al-Farabi — polymath from Otrar, Kazakhstan. Called Muallim Thani (the “Second Teacher” after Aristotle). Worked in philosophy, mathematics, music theory, logic, political philosophy, and ethics. Key works: The Great Book of Music, Treatise on the Views of the Residents of the Virtuous City, On the Classification of Sciences. Synthesized Greek, Arab, Persian, Indian, and Turkic intellectual traditions.

Sufism (Tasawwuf)

Islamic mystical belief and practice seeking divine love and knowledge through direct personal experience of God. The Arabic name tasawwuf means “dress in wool” (suf = wool; early ascetics wore simple wool garments). Sufis are also called fuqara (the poor, emphasizing spiritual poverty) or in Persian darvish. Not a single path but various mystical orders (tarikas), all sharing the goal of understanding God and experiencing divine love.

Khoja Ahmed Yasawi (11th–12th c.)

The most prominent Sufi preacher in the Turkic world. Founded the Yasawi Sufi order. His masterwork Diwan-i Hikmet (Book of Wisdom) was written in the Turkic language at a time when local literature was exclusively in Persian — a revolutionary choice making Sufi wisdom accessible to ordinary people. So popular that young people memorized it entirely; it was called the “Turkish Koran” or “Second Great Book.” His mausoleum in Turkestan (built by Timur in the 14th c.) is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Sufi Tarikas in Kazakhstan

Three major Sufi orders present in Kazakhstan: Yasawiyya (founded by Khoja Ahmed Yasawi; followed by Kazakhs, Uzbeks, Uyghurs), Naqshbandiyya (known for silent dhikr; also widespread among Kazakhs), Qadiriyya (one of the oldest orders, founded in Baghdad; more common among Caucasian communities in Kazakhstan).

Dhikr

Islamic spiritual practice involving the repeated chanting of prayers glorifying God. Performed after formal prayers, during religious gatherings. Developed as a meditative Sufi practice. In Kazakhstan, the “loud dhikr” tradition combined chanting, singing, and even dancing — reflecting both mystical devotion and shamanistic roots of Kazakh spirituality.

Silk Road Cities of Kazakhstan

Key medieval urban centers: Otrar (Farab) — birthplace of Al-Farabi; minting city; destroyed by Mongols but rebuilt. Turkistan (Yasy/Shavgar) — spiritual center; home of Yasawi; 1500+ years old. Taraz — major trade center. Isfijab — frontier city with covered markets. Balasagun — capital of Karakhanid Khanate; in the Chui Valley. These cities linked nomadic steppe and agricultural oasis, East and West on the Silk Road.

C. Expanded Academic Notes

The Role of Sufism in Central Asian Society

Sufism was not merely a private spiritual discipline. In Central Asia it was a major social, cultural, and political force:

  • Educational role: Sufi orders educated the masses, instilling moral and ethical values alongside spiritual principles
  • Missionary role: Sufis spread Islam throughout Central Asia, South Asia, Africa, and parts of Europe through peaceful, compassionate teaching
  • Literary impact: Sufi vocabulary and metaphors permeated Arabic, Persian, Turkic, Urdu, Pashto, Punjabi, and Sindhi literature; mystical poetry reached ordinary people who lacked access to formal education
  • Political role: Some Sufi leaders established local authority, served as intermediaries between rulers and people, and in some cases founded dynasties
  • Cultural syncretism in Kazakhstan: Sufism blended with pre-Islamic Kazakh beliefs (especially shamanism), creating a uniquely Kazakh spiritual tradition. The “loud dhikr” with singing and dancing reflects this fusion
Al-Farabi: Bridging East and West

Al-Farabi (870–950) stands as one of the greatest thinkers of the medieval Islamic Golden Age. Born in Otrar (present-day southern Kazakhstan), he traveled to Baghdad, Damascus, Cairo, Bukhara, Alexandria, and other centers of learning.

His Treatise on the Views of the Residents of the Virtuous City (Ara Ahl al-Madina al-Fadila) is a political philosophy masterwork describing the ideal Islamic city-state governed by reason and virtue — directly inspired by Plato’s Republic but adapted to the Islamic context.

His title Muallim Thani (Second Teacher) reflected his status as the inheritor of Aristotle’s logical tradition. His works on the classification of sciences organized all human knowledge for the Islamic world. His Kitab al-Musiqi al-Kabir (Great Book of Music) remains one of the most comprehensive treatises on music theory from the medieval period.

Al-Farabi synthesized Greek, Arab, Persian, Indian, and Turkic intellectual traditions — making him one of history’s great cross-cultural intellects. His manuscripts are preserved in libraries worldwide, and “Farabianism” has developed as a dedicated field of scholarship.

Post-Mongol Cultural Revival

The Mongol invasion (early 13th century) devastated Central Asian urban culture — Otrar was destroyed during the invasion (1219), killing 60,000 people. However, cities showed remarkable resilience:

  • By the mid-13th century, cities began to revive; Otrar’s mint restarted
  • New centers emerged: Sarayshuk, Sairan, Saudakent, Baruket, Kumkent, Suzak
  • Under Timur (Tamerlane), the annexation of southern Kazakhstan (late 14th c.) brought architectural revival: the mausoleum of Ahmed Yasawi in Turkestan, Arstambap Mosque
  • Turkestan rose to become a major cultural and spiritual center under the Timurid and later Kazakh Khanate
Contemporary Sufism in Kazakhstan

Despite limited academic study, Sufism remains deeply embedded in Kazakh spiritual life:

  • Pilgrimage culture: Shrines at Khoja Ahmed Yasawi (Turkestan), Arstambap, and Biketata remain active pilgrimage sites
  • Pre-Islamic persistence: Family cycle rites (eating baursak for family well-being), ancestor worship, three-day cooking bans after deaths, oil-pouring rituals (otkamaikyu), Nauryz preparations
  • Pseudo-Sufi challenge: Lack of scholarly attention has allowed pseudo-Sufi movements to emerge, misrepresenting authentic traditions
  • Religious renaissance: A 2021 study by Timur Bayevar documented a growing spiritual revival, marked increase in pilgrimage to sacred shrines, and veneration of saints even after Soviet suppression
  • Political tension: While Kazakh political leaders rhetorically embrace Yasawi’s legacy, Salafi influences tend to dominate official religious policy (Emilanova observation)

Lecture 05

Formation of Kazakh Culture. Kazakh Culture in the XVIII – Late XIX Century

The Kazakh Khanate, oral poetry traditions, biy law, and the Jyraus who carried the nation’s soul

Kazakh Khanate (1465) Jyrau Poetry Oral Epics Biy (Justice) Tengriism-Islam Syncretism Jungar Invasions

A. Lecture Overview

The Kazakh Khanate (founded 1465) provided the political framework within which Kazakh culture crystallized as a distinct civilization. This period (roughly 15th–19th centuries) saw the florescence of Kazakh oral poetry (tolğau, epics, ritual songs), the institution of jyraus (singer-sages who combined artistic, philosophical, and political functions), and the complex process of Islamic-Tengriist cultural syncretism.

The catastrophic Jungar invasions of the 18th century (Aqtaban Shubyryndy — the “Great Disaster”) profoundly shaped Kazakh collective memory and produced some of the most powerful jyrau poetry of the era.

B. Core Concepts & Definitions

Kazakh Khanate (1465)

Founded by Janiybek Khan and Kerei Khan after their separation from the Abulkhayr Khanate. The first independent Kazakh state, which provided the political and social framework for Kazakh cultural consolidation. By the 17th–18th centuries, the Khanate encompassed most of the present Kazakh steppe.

Jyrau (Жырау)

Singer-sages who were the dominant cultural institution of the 15th–18th centuries. Jyraus combined the roles of poet, philosopher, political advisor, and social conscience. They composed tolğau (reflective songs about life, death, ethics), memorial songs (joqtau), and epic poems. They held high social status and often advised khans on significant state problems.

Tolğau

An instructive, reflective genre of Kazakh oral poetry about life, death, the present and future, expressing wisdom, moral values, and philosophical insight. Often addressed to khans and community leaders, containing social commentary, ethical guidance, and philosophical reflection. The main genre of jyrau poetry.

Genres of Kazakh Oral Poetry

The rich tradition included: Tolğau (reflective/instructive songs), Ritual songs (besik zhyry/lullabies, sandugash, bitazar, jarjar, joqtau), Proverbs & sayings, Fairy tales (ertegi), Heroic epics (Koblandy Batyr, Alpamys, Er Tarğyn, Qambar Batyr), Lyrical epics (Qozy Körpesh & Bayan Sulu, Qyz Zhibek).

Batyr (Warrior) Institution

Batyrs were a distinct social group within Kazakh society with their own ideology, lifestyle, military traditions, rules, and ethics. Specialized exclusively in military art. Their values and deeds are preserved in Kazakh epic literature, especially heroic epics. The batyr embodied the Kazakh ideals of courage, sacrifice, and homeland defense.

Aqtaban Shubyryndy (Great Disaster)

The catastrophic Kalmyk-Jungar invasions of the 18th century that nearly destroyed the Kazakh people — referred to in Kazakh historical memory as “Aqtaban Shubyryndy, Alqakol Sulama” (Running on bare feet, lying down at Alqakol). This trauma produced some of the most powerful jyrau poetry (Umbetej Jyrau’s memorial songs for Bogenbay Batyr).

Tengriism-Islam Syncretism in Kazakh Culture

The process by which Islamic beliefs were absorbed into an existing Tengriist framework, creating a distinctly Kazakh spiritual identity. Evidence: the phrase “Allah-Tangry berdi” (God and Tengri gave it) — combining Islamic Allah with indigenous Tengri. Marriage could be recognized without a mullah through community approval and the jarjar choral wedding song. The concept of Tengri persisted in Kazakh language and worldview long after formal Islamization.

Marital Customs (15th–17th c.)

Despite Islam’s recognition of marriage as a sacred contract requiring a mullah, in Kazakh society religious officiation was not always required. Marriage could be recognized through: (1) community approval, (2) performance of jarjar (choral wedding song). These weddings were social and cultural events rooted in oral tradition and communal participation rather than formal Islamic procedure.

C. Expanded Academic Notes

The Jyrau Tradition: Political Poets of the Steppe

The jyrau tradition represents one of Central Asia’s most distinctive cultural institutions. Unlike court poets in sedentary civilizations, Kazakh jyraus were politically independent figures who could challenge khans with moral authority. Their tolğau poetry addressed the great questions of their time: tribal unity, state power, ethics, honor, and the meaning of life.

Key Jyraus and their periods:

  • Asanqaıghy (11th–12th c.) — proto-jyrau figure; known for wisdom sayings about the ideal homeland
  • Shalqiyz Jyrau (15th c.)
  • Dospan Bet Jyrau (16th c.)
  • Jiyembet Jyrau (17th c.)
  • Aqtamberdi Jyrau (17th–18th c.)
  • Bukhar Jyrau (17th–18th c.) — advisor to Khan Ablai
  • Umbetej Jyrau (17th–18th c.) — composed famous joqtau (memorial song) for Bogenbay Batyr

The jyraus’ main themes: cohesion and unity of the three Kazakh zhuzes (tribal confederations), strengthening of Khanate military power, ethics and morality, honor and dignity.

Heroic vs. Lyrical Epics in Kazakh Tradition

Heroic epics (Batyrlyk jyr): Dominated 16th–18th century cultural life. Examples: Koblandy Batyr, Alpamys, Er Tarğyn, Qambar Batyr. Reflected values of courage, sacrifice, and homeland defense. Passed from generation to generation through oral performance.

Lyrical epics (Ghashyqtyq jyr): Qozy Körpesh & Bayan Sulu (Kazakh Romeo and Juliet), Qyz Zhibek, Husain and Khushyaq, Sulushash. Represent romantic and emotional dimensions, reflecting love, beauty, social codes governing relationships, honor, and reputation.

Both types preserve Kazakh collective memory, social values, and historical experiences in performative oral form.

Lecture 06

Kazakh Culture in the XX Century

Soviet transformation, cultural revolution, and the paradox of development under totalitarianism

Soviet Period Mukhtar Auezov Academy of Sciences Opera & Ballet Cinema Ideological Dictate Post-Independence

A. Lecture Overview

The 20th century brought the most dramatic transformations in Kazakh cultural history. Soviet rule (1920–1991) was deeply paradoxical: it simultaneously destroyed traditional nomadic life (through forced sedentarization and collectivization), yet invested significantly in developing Kazakh literature, theater, opera, fine arts, cinema, and scientific institutions — all within a framework of ideological control and socialist realist aesthetics.

Key tensions: state-directed cultural production vs. authentic national expression; Russian-language dominance vs. Kazakh language development; Western artistic genres vs. Kazakh folk traditions. The December 16, 1991 independence marked the beginning of a new cultural stage.

B. Core Concepts & Definitions

Socialist Realism

The official artistic doctrine of the Soviet Union: art must depict reality in its “revolutionary development,” serving the goals of socialist construction and the Communist Party. In practice, this meant glorifying Soviet achievements, depicting idealized workers and heroes, and avoiding modernist experimentation. Kazakh artists worked within (and sometimes against) this framework.

Alash Orda

The Kazakh autonomist movement (1917–1920) that sought to establish a Kazakh state after the Russian Revolution. Its intellectuals (Alikhan Bokeikhanov, Akhmet Baitursynov, Mirzhaqyp Dulatov) were foundational figures in Kazakh cultural nationalism. Most were later repressed or executed by the Soviets. Their cultural contribution — development of Kazakh alphabet, literature, and identity — laid the foundation for 20th century Kazakh culture.

Mukhtar Auezov (1897–1961)

The greatest Kazakh writer of the 20th century. Author of the monumental novel Abay Zholy (Path of Abai), which received the Stalin Prize First Degree. Called an “Encyclopedia of Kazakh People’s Lives,” it depicts the life of the great poet Abai Qunanbaiuly against the backdrop of 19th century Kazakh steppe life, traditions, and social transformation.

Academy of Sciences of the Kazakh SSR

Established in the Soviet period to develop Kazakh scientific research. Its presidents included Qanysh Satpayev (metallurgist, first Kazakh president of the Academy), Mohammed Qonayev, Shokhan Shokin, and others. The Academy produced a two-volume history of Kazakhstan and conducted in-depth research on Kazakh folklore, literature, and linguistics. Developed over 100 high-yield crop varieties in agriculture.

Baurzhan Momyshuly (1910–1982)

Hero of World War II and writer. Led a division in the Battle of Moscow (November 1941) as part of Panfilov’s division. Inventor of the “spiritual spiral of Baurzhan” — a battle tactic connecting back to Saka warrior methods. His experience was fictionalized in Alexander Bek’s novel Volokolamsk Highway. His tactics were studied in military universities in the USA, Cuba, and Israel. Also a writer who addressed moral-ethical issues of youth and patriotism.

Development of Kazakh Theater & Opera

Soviet period introduced European artistic genres into Kazakh culture: the musical theater evolved into the Kazakh State Opera and Ballet Theater. The first Kazakh opera Birzhan Sara was written by Akhmet Zhubanov and Latif Khamidi (1946). Famous Kazakh opera singers: Rozha Baglanova, Jamilya Maqpirova, Bibi-Gul Tulegenova. Russian artist Nikolai Khludov opened a painting studio in Almaty in 1920, initiating Kazakh fine arts.

Kazakh Cinema

The first Kazakh film Aman Geldi (1938) depicted the 1916 rebellion (when Tsarism tried to conscript Kazakhs for WWI, triggering the Zhaqtap uprising). Soviet-era cinema followed socialist realist principles but also preserved historical Kazakh material. Post-independence, the “Kazakh New Wave” emerged with directors like Ardak Amirqalov exploring authentic national themes.

Contradictory Trends in Soviet Kazakh Culture

Soviet cultural policy was inherently contradictory: it promoted literacy, education, arts, and sciences (genuine cultural development) while simultaneously imposing ideological censorship, destroying traditional nomadic life, and executing Kazakh intellectuals (Stalinist repressions). As stated in the lecture: “In the difficult conditions of totalitarianism and ideological dictate, an integral part of spiritual values has developed.”

C. Expanded Academic Notes

Periodization of Soviet Kazakh Cultural Development

Pre-War Period (1920s–early 1940s): Main themes: establishment of Kazakh state, national unity, historical consciousness. Development of Kazakh alphabet (shifted from Arabic → Latin → Cyrillic), literacy campaigns, first newspapers and journals in Kazakh.

World War II Period (1941–1945): War heroism became the dominant cultural theme. Writers and poets celebrated Kazakh fighters; Baurzhan Momyshuly became a national hero. Auezov worked on Abay Zholy.

Post-War Period (1945–1991): Reconstruction themes; expanded institutional development (Academy of Sciences, 1946); flourishing of Kazakh opera, theater, cinema, and fine arts. The 1958 Moscow exhibition of Kazakh artwork showed 500+ paintings. By the 1980s, the first stirrings of national cultural revival (December 1986 Jeltoqsan protests).

  • Gabit Musrepov — playwright and prose writer
  • Iliyas Esenberlin — historical trilogy Nomads about the Kazakh Khanate
  • Baurzhan Momyshuly — war hero turned writer; moral-ethical themes

Lecture 07

Kazakh Culture in the Context of Modern World Processes

Globalization, identity crisis, and the explosive energy of post-independence Kazakh contemporary art

Globalization Identity Contemporary Art Post-Soviet Art Mass Culture Digital Nomadism

A. Lecture Overview

This lecture examines the intersection of globalization and Kazakh cultural identity in the post-independence era. It explores two major questions: (1) how globalization challenges and reshapes national and ethnic identity, and (2) how Kazakh contemporary art emerged after 1991 as a powerful expression of social reality, historical trauma, and cultural search.

The period after 1991 saw artists liberated from Soviet censorship but simultaneously facing economic collapse, identity crisis, and the challenge of finding new cultural languages in a globalized world.

B. Core Concepts & Definitions

Globalization

The formation and extension of relationships and interdependence of countries in different areas: economy, military, finance, information, and advanced technologies. Main idea: removing boundaries between countries; creating equal opportunities. Based on: internationalization of capital, development of information technologies, formation of global information space. Some scholars trace its origins to medieval trade; others to 20th century capitalism.

Identity (Cultural)

A social relation involving: (1) a system of material and spiritual cultural values that distinguishes one society from others, and (2) self-awareness by individuals and groups of their equality with others and their qualitative uniqueness. Positive identity: “We are like them” (solidarity). Negative identity: “We are different/better” (exclusion, potential xenophobia). Types: personal, state, collective, gender, sexual, ethnic, national.

Two Critical Cultural Trends in Globalization

1. Localization of national-state culture: how nations survive as nations, preserve uniqueness. 2. Integration of various cultures into a single planetary culture. These tensions define contemporary cultural politics worldwide and in Kazakhstan specifically.

Global Trends in Modern Civilization

Formation of information society; emergence of modernist trends in art; manifestation of mass culture (spreading faster than elite culture); increasing influence of religion on worldview; aggravation of global problems (environmental, peace/war).

Types of Culture in Modern Kazakhstan

Based on degree of prevalence and rootedness of values: (1) Known vs. Unknown culture — known culture is widely circulated and accepted; unknown culture exists in past or marginal niches. (2) Culture of individual, social groups, and society — generational differences (youth vs. adult culture in music, literature, cinema). (3) Capital vs. Local culture — urban/modern concentrated in cities; folk/mythological preserved in rural areas.

Post-Independence Kazakh Contemporary Art

After 1991, Kazakh artists experienced liberation from Soviet censorship but faced: economic collapse (loss of state commissions), identity vacuum (Soviet goals gone; no new framework), and isolation from global art markets. Responded by: experimentation, new genres (installation, performance, photography), exploration of historical trauma, national identity, and social marginalization.

Key Post-Independence Artists

Rustam Khalfin: minimalist paintings depicting the headless, marginal person of the independence era — trapped between two walls (past and future). Sergei Maslov: surreal, non-realistic paintings mixing modern reality with Kazakh national culture (old man playing kobyz). Moldaqul Narymbetov: installation artist using rubber tires and unconventional materials; “Three Bees” painting (3 colors only). Saule Suleymanova: painter using plastic bags as medium; documented 1986 Jeltoqsan protests, 1930s famine (Iskhod Kazakhov), Karolak era.

Lev Gumilev & Passionarity

Lev Gumilev described identity as “the objective basis for concentration and mobilization — the passionate (passionate force) of society.” Insufficient knowledge of one’s identity leads to hostile emotions, xenophobia, and aggressiveness. Education, including religious literacy, is the solution to negative identity formation.

C. Expanded Academic Notes

The Marginal Person in Post-Soviet Kazakh Art

Rustam Khalfin’s famous painting of a headless figure standing between two walls became an iconic image of the post-Soviet condition in Kazakhstan. The analysis offered in the lecture is profound: the headless figure represents a person without goals, without direction — because in Soviet times, the government provided all goals and directions. When the USSR collapsed, people were left without a prescribed future, without a past they could claim, and without a present they understood. The figure is “marginal” — belonging neither here nor there. Three colors: white, yellow, black — minimalist but powerful.

This artistic interpretation connects to the sociological concept of marginalization — when individuals or groups lose their established place in social structures and find themselves between worlds.

Saule Suleymanova’s Plastic Bag Medium

Saule Suleymanova’s choice of plastic bags as her primary artistic medium is both conceptually and materially significant. She has documented:

  • The Karolak period (deportations, repressions)
  • The 1930s famine in Kazakhstan (Iskhod Kazakhov — Exodus of Kazakhs)
  • The December 1986 Jeltoqsan student protests in Almaty (using only black and white bags)
  • Her recent project Between Us Girls — ironic reuse of negative comments about her work

The plastic bag as medium: disposable, common, fragile — yet she transforms it into permanent art about impermanent but devastating historical moments. A semiotic analysis: the bag (everyday symbol of commercial culture) becomes a sign of historical trauma and survival.

Lecture 08

Kazakh Culture in the Context of Globalization

Museums, cultural institutions, and Hofstede’s cultural dimensions in the age of global interconnection

Museums Cultural Memory Hofstede Theory Cultural Dimensions Cultural Institutions Heritage Programs

A. Lecture Overview

This lecture examines three major questions: (1) the role of museums in preserving cultural and ethnic memory; (2) the role of cultural institutions (theaters, libraries, etc.) in building the “cult of knowledge, pragmatism, and competitiveness” among modern Kazakhstanis; and (3) Geert Hofstede’s cultural dimensions theory — a powerful analytical tool for comparing Kazakhstan’s cultural profile with other nations.

B. Core Concepts & Definitions

Museums as Memory Institutions

Museums preserve cultural and ethnic memory through exhibits and artifacts. In Kazakhstan, the state program Cultural Heritage has driven museum development. However, inequality exists: museums in Astana and Almaty have significantly more resources than regional museums. Global trend: museums shifting from “temple” model (passive display) to interactive, democratic venues where visitors touch, experience, and participate.

Cultural Institutions (Definition)

Institutions that engage people in cultural life, interpret and disseminate cultural, scientific, and environmental knowledge. Examples: museums, libraries, historical and botanical societies, community cultural centers, archives, churches, mosques, arts centers. They help realize local and state cultural programs and create supplementary education opportunities.

Hofstede’s Cultural Dimensions Theory

Developed by Dutch social psychologist Geert Hofstede based on IBM employee surveys across 50+ countries (late 1970s). The internationally recognized standard for comparing cultural differences. Originally 4 dimensions (later expanded to 6): Power Distance Index, Individualism vs. Collectivism, Masculinity vs. Femininity, Uncertainty Avoidance Index, Long vs. Short-Term Orientation, Indulgence vs. Restraint.

Power Distance Index (PDI)

The degree to which less powerful members of society accept unequal distribution of power. High PDI (Kazakhstan): hierarchical relationships; students stand when teacher enters; children obey parents without question; employees need boss approval; unequal distribution of power is accepted. Low PDI (USA, Scandinavia): flat hierarchies; teachers can be wrong; equal rights; bosses accessible.

Individualism vs. Collectivism

Collectivist (Kazakhstan): achievements are shared; loyalty to group; group defends members; responsibility for others’ well-being. Individualist (USA, Western Europe): personal responsibility; weak ties outside core family; individual achievement celebrated.

Masculinity vs. Femininity

Masculine societies: distinct gender roles; men assertive and ambitious; even women tend toward assertiveness and career-focus. Feminine societies: overlapping gender roles; modesty as virtue. Kazakhstan: approximately 50/50, though perceived by many as more masculine.

Uncertainty Avoidance Index (UAI)

How well people cope with anxiety about the unknown. High UAI (Kazakhstan): preference for predictable, controllable life; rules and structure comforting; when life feels uncontrollable, resignation or religious fatalism. Slows innovation adoption. Low UAI: relaxed, open, inclusive; willing to experiment.

Long vs. Short-Term Orientation

Long-term (Kazakhstan: Vision 2030, 2050): pragmatic, thrifty, patient; plans for decades ahead. Short-term: emphasis on principles, consistency, truth; religious and nationalistic; seeks quick results.

Indulgence vs. Restraint

Indulgent (high score): free expression of drives and emotions; enjoying life, fun, holidays. Restrained (Kazakhstan, Russia): suppression of gratification; strict social norms; behavior regulated by social expectations rather than personal desires.

Kazakhstan’s Hofstede Profile

Kazakhstan scores: High PDI (hierarchical society), Collectivist (group over individual), 50/50 Masculinity/Femininity, High UAI (innovation-resistant, risk-averse structurally), Long-term Oriented (Vision 2030/2050), Restrained (strict social norms, limited emotional expression).

C. Expanded Academic Notes

Hofstede’s Six Dimensions Applied to Kazakhstan

Understanding Kazakhstan through Hofstede helps explain many cultural phenomena:

  • High PDI: Students stand when teachers enter; children follow parents’ career choices; hierarchical workplace communication; bosses have private offices
  • Collectivism: Success is communal (“my achievement belongs to those who helped me”); group decision-making; strong clan (ru) loyalty
  • High UAI: Slow adoption of new technologies and business models; preference for established practices; comfort with rules and bureaucracy; religious fatalism when control feels impossible
  • Long-term orientation: National development programs (Vision 2030, Kazakhstan-2050) are culturally consistent with long-term planning orientation
  • Restraint: Strong social norms govern behavior; public emotional expression constrained; personal desires subordinated to social expectations

Critical note from lecture: Uncertainty avoidance does NOT mean risk avoidance; rather, it means that in high-UAI cultures, innovation is more difficult to introduce and development is slower. This has direct implications for the “Digital Kazakhstan” program and IT sector development.

Interactive Museum Revolution

The global shift from “museum as temple” to “museum as experience space” is critically important for Kazakhstan’s cultural development. Traditional Kazakh museums kept artifacts behind glass, passive and untouchable. The global trend:

  • Visitors can touch, sit in, and interact with artifacts
  • Technology (screens, audio, multimedia, touchscreens) enhances immersion
  • Visitors can contribute their own items and memories (participatory museum)
  • Museums serve as urban creative sites for tourism and education

Example from lecture: Kokshetau museum exposition of 16th-century flood where visitors could touch items and even donate to the collection. Personal initiative in museum creation: Maken Terigildin’s private Museum of Mining and Smelting History in Karaganda — showing that cultural preservation doesn’t require state initiative.

Lecture 09

The Cultural Policies of Kazakhstan

State cultural strategy, national identity design, multiculturalism, and the lived experience of cultural shock

Cultural Policy Kazakhstani Identity Modernization Multiculturalism Cultural Shock Digital Kazakhstan

A. Lecture Overview

Kazakhstan’s cultural policy represents the state’s systematic effort to define and project national identity in a post-Soviet, globalized world. This lecture covers: the official Kazakhstan Model of Cultural Policy (its goals, debates, and conceptual framework); the tension between modernization and multiculturalism as the two theoretical schools driving policy; and the individual experience of cultural shock when entering a new cultural environment.

B. Core Concepts & Definitions

Kazakhstan Model of Cultural Policy

The Concept of Cultural Policy of the Republic of Kazakhstan is a state program defining national cultural development. Its objectives: (1) formation of spiritual and moral guidelines and new Kazakhstani patriotism; (2) development of the unified Kazakhstan cultural space preserving national diversity; (3) integration of national culture into global cultural space; (4) creation of a competitive cultural environment; (5) preservation and popularization of historical and cultural heritage; (6) use of cultural landscape for tourism development.

Kazakhstani Identity (vs. Kazakh Identity)

The state has deliberately promoted Kazakhstani (Kazakhstan-based, civic) identity over Kazakh (ethnic) identity — reflecting the multi-ethnic composition of Kazakhstan (Kazakhs, Russians, Ukrainians, Uzbeks, Uyghurs, Germans, Koreans, etc.). The goal: “formation of a Kazakhstani nation unified by ideals and values.” Debates mirror multiculturalism debates in Western Europe.

Modernization Theory (in Cultural Policy)

The concept of social, political, economic, and cultural modernization through state-led initiatives. Popular in developing countries in the 1960s–70s. Kazakhstan attempts to frame its post-independence reforms within this framework. Key state-driven modernization initiatives: infrastructure development, language reform (Kazakh language Latinization), Digital Kazakhstan program, Vision 2030/2050.

Multiculturalism

A framework for managing cultural diversity within a state — recognizing and preserving distinct cultural identities while maintaining social cohesion. Kazakhstan’s challenges mirror Western European debates: how to address ethnic and religious minorities, handle changing cultural landscapes, and integrate a large number of recent migrants (60% population growth 1991–2050 from Caucasia, Afghanistan, China, Iran, Kyrgyzstan, Uzbekistan).

Cultural Shock

Defined by Canadian anthropologist Kalervo Oberg (mid-1950s) as “the anxiety that results from losing all our familiar signs and symbols of social intercourse.” Occurs when a person leaves their familiar cultural environment and enters a new one — as in international student mobility (academic exchange). A person is not born with culture but acquires it; when familiar cultural frameworks are removed, disorientation results.

Four Stages of Cultural Shock (Oberg)

1. Honeymoon Stage: euphoria, enthusiasm, everything seems good, feel understood and understanding. 2. Rejection/Regression Stage: physical tiredness, health problems, language difficulties, missing home, comparing cultures negatively, feeling confused/lonely/anxious. 3. Adjustment/Negotiation Stage: developing routines, making friends, understanding differences non-judgmentally, adapting. 4. Mastery Stage: feels like a cultural native (takes 2–5+ years; not all reach this stage).

Eight Factors Affecting Cultural Shock Level

1. Previous intercultural experience; 2. Prior knowledge of target culture; 3. Linguistic ability in target language; 4. Previously learned human values; 5. Personality traits; 6. Cultural similarities between origin and target; 7. Geography and climate differences; 8. Situational factors in the new environment.

Nationality vs. National Identity

Nationality refers to legal-political membership in a state. National identity is the cultural, historical, and emotional sense of belonging to a nation. Kazakhstan’s policy specifically addresses how to build a shared Kazakhstani national identity that transcends ethnic divisions while preserving ethnic cultural diversity.

C. Expanded Academic Notes

The Eurasian Context of Kazakhstan’s Cultural Policy

Kazakhstan’s cultural policy debates are shaped by several geopolitical realities:

  • Eurasian Economic Union: Kazakhstan became a member (with Russia and Belarus) in 2010, introducing free movement of people and goods. This creates pressure on cultural distinctiveness while enabling economic integration.
  • Migration dynamics: Since 1991, large population movements from Caucasia, Afghanistan, China, Iran, Kyrgyzstan, and Uzbekistan have transformed Kazakhstan’s ethnic composition. Oralmans (ethnic Kazakhs repatriated from abroad, especially Mongolia and China) add another dimension.
  • Language policy: The shift from Cyrillic to Latin alphabet for Kazakh (announced 2017, ongoing) is a major cultural-political gesture signaling Western orientation, break from Soviet past, and alignment with Turkic world.
  • Soft power: The state uses cultural heritage, international festivals, UNESCO applications, and cultural diplomacy to project Kazakh culture globally.
Cultural Shock in Practice: Student Mobility

The lecture frames cultural shock in the context of student academic mobility — directly relevant to Astana IT University students who participate in international exchange programs. Key insights for Kazakh students abroad:

  • Kazakhstan has HIGH Power Distance and Collectivist culture; many Western universities have LOW Power Distance (questioning professors is normal) and INDIVIDUALIST culture (independent study expected)
  • The transition can be jarring: from a culture where teachers are authorities to one where intellectual challenge is encouraged
  • The Rejection Stage (Stage 2) typically occurs 1–3 months in — important to know and prepare for
  • Mastery Stage can take 2–5 years — short exchanges rarely reach full cultural integration

Lecture 10

Cultural Heritage of Kazakhstan: Tradition, Innovation, and Identity in a Globalized World

Cultural landscapes, nature-culture ecology, heritage resilience, and Kazakhstan’s living cultural future

A. Lecture Overview

This final lecture synthesizes the entire course by examining Kazakhstan’s cultural heritage not as a museum artifact but as a living, adaptive system. It introduces the concept of cultural landscapes (UNESCO) — where nature and human culture are inseparably intertwined — and explores how Kazakhstan can navigate the challenges of modernization and globalization while preserving its distinctive cultural identity.

Key insight: “Cultural resilience is not just about protecting sites or artifacts — it’s about protecting the system of knowledge, the rituals and landscape, and the stories that define who we are.”

B. Core Concepts & Definitions

Cultural Ecology

Julian Steward’s concept (expanded by Michael Berourb) that national identity includes an understanding of place and landscape as integral to belonging. In Kazakhstan: national identity is tied to geography (white steppe, deserts, lakes, mountains) which shaped traditions, economy, and social structures. Kazakh nomadic lifestyle is cultural ecology in action.

Cultural Landscapes (UNESCO)

According to UNESCO’s Operational Guidelines, cultural landscapes are “cultural properties that represent the combined works of nature and of man.” Three types: Designed/Created (intentionally shaped: Mausoleum of Khoja Ahmed Yasawi, Great Wall of China), Organically Evolved (developed through long-term human-nature interaction: Kazakh steppe, Machu Picchu), Associative (significant cultural/spiritual/symbolic value: Ulytau in Kazakhstan, Stonehenge in UK).

Ulytau (Ұлытау)

A sacred and historically significant region in central Kazakhstan — an associative cultural landscape. Mountains, rivers, and ancient burial mounds carry symbolic value linked to Kazakh myths and the nation’s ancestral past. Site of coronations, khan assemblies, and major historical events. Now a UNESCO-recognized site of cultural significance.

Cultural Resilience

The capacity of a culture to adapt, maintain core identity, and continue transmitting values and practices across generations even in the face of disruption (colonization, globalization, environmental change, forced assimilation). For Kazakhstan: maintaining the living practice of traditions, not just their historical documentation.

Hybrid Model (Tradition + Innovation)

The course’s concluding framework: globalization brings both opportunities and pressures; by embracing a hybrid model where innovation revitalizes tradition, Kazakhstan can preserve its identity while engaging confidently with the global community. Traditional ethno-cultural codes are acquiring new and relevant forms (e.g., ethnic style in contemporary fashion, design, and art).

Kazakh Cultural Practices Linked to Nature

Yurt & nomadic lifestyle: portable dwelling perfectly adapted to the steppe; reflects sophisticated understanding of natural landscape and climate. Horse culture & Tengriism: horses = transport, food, and symbolic meaning; reverence for horses tied to ancient sky and nature worship. Nauryz: spring festival tied to nature’s rhythms; symbolizes renewal, fertility, and community harmony with seasonal change. Traditional ecological knowledge: centuries of pastoralist expertise in sustainable animal husbandry, water use, and pasture management.

Modernization Challenges to Nature-Culture Balance

Urbanization reducing grazing lands; pollution and climate change threatening sacred ecosystems; loss of biodiversity impacting traditional knowledge (medicinal plant practices). Response: Kazakhstan integrates heritage preservation with environmental protection through UNESCO World Heritage designations (Cold Winter Deserts of Turan; Saryarka; Tien Shan).

Institutions of Cultural Memory

Carriers of cultural memory in Kazakhstan: Cultural institutions (museums, cultural centers, craft programs — needlework, felt-making, yurt construction). Educational institutions (schools and universities for folklore, traditional arts, Kazakh language). Government initiatives (textbooks, films, digitization campaigns, cultural literacy programs).

C. Expanded Academic Notes

Three Types of Cultural Landscapes: Deep Analysis

1. Designed/Created Landscapes

Intentionally planned for specific purposes. The Mausoleum of Khoja Ahmed Yasawi in Turkestan (built by Timur, late 14th c.) was planned as a religious and cultural center — the surrounding area was intentionally laid out for pilgrimage, contemplation, and social gathering. The Great Wall of China shapes, defines, and culturally marks a vast geographical boundary.

2. Organically Evolved Landscapes

Developed gradually through long-term human-environment interaction. The Kazakh steppe is the world’s most extensive grassland ecosystem, shaped over millennia by nomadic pastoralism — the grazing patterns, migration routes, water management, and sacred site locations all reflect centuries of human ecological knowledge embedded in the landscape itself. Machu Picchu (Peru) similarly shows how Inca civilization adapted to an extreme mountain environment.

3. Associative Landscapes

These hold power through their cultural meanings rather than physical modification. Ulytau in central Kazakhstan is associated with the founding of the Kazakh Khanate, coronation ceremonies of khans, and the spiritual heart of Kazakh ancestral memory. The mountains and rivers here are not just geography — they are repositories of collective memory. Stonehenge (UK) similarly derives its significance from prehistoric ritual and astronomical practice rather than structural modification of the landscape.

Kazakhstan’s UNESCO World Heritage Sites and Cultural Memory

Kazakhstan’s UNESCO-recognized sites as of the course:

  • Mausoleum of Khoja Ahmed Yasawi (Turkestan) — Cultural Heritage, inscribed 2003
  • Petroglyphs of Tamgaly — Cultural Heritage
  • Saryarka — Steppe and Lakes of Northern Kazakhstan — Natural Heritage
  • Cold Winter Deserts of Turan — Natural Heritage (cross-border)
  • Western Tian-Shan — Natural Heritage (cross-border)

The dual recognition of cultural and natural sites reflects the UNESCO principle that nature and culture cannot be separated in Kazakhstan’s heritage — the physical environment IS the cultural heritage.

The final lecture’s concluding message synthesizes the entire course:

“Kazakhstan’s cultural heritage is not something frozen in the past. It’s living, adaptive and deeply embedded in the land, the language and the people. Globalization brings both opportunities and pressures. But by embracing a hybrid model where innovation revitalizes tradition, Kazakhstan can preserve its identity while confidently engaging with the global community. And most importantly, cultural resilience is not just about protecting sites or artifacts, it’s about protecting the system of knowledge, the rituals and landscape and the stories that define who we are.”

This connects back to Lecture 1’s definition of culture as a “superbiological means of adaptation” — culture is not a fixed heritage but a living adaptive system. F. Real-World Examples

🇰🇿 Ulytau: Associative Landscape as Living Memory

Ulytau in central Kazakhstan is not a single monument but an entire region laden with historical and spiritual significance. Here the Great Khans assembled; here Genghis Khan’s sons divided the steppe world. Ancient burial mounds (kurgans), rock inscriptions, and sacred springs make the landscape itself a text — in Lotman’s semiotic sense. Contemporary Kazakhs who visit Ulytau often report a profound sense of ancestral connection — the land transmits cultural memory without words.

🇰🇿 Nauryz as Living Cultural Ecology

Nauryz (March 22 — the spring equinox) is the perfect illustration of cultural ecology: it synchronizes human community rhythms with natural cycles. The cleaning of homes mirrors nature’s renewal; the preparation of nauryz kozhe (seven-ingredient dish) ritually incorporates the land’s bounty. After being suppressed during the Soviet era, Nauryz was revived after independence and is now a national holiday — a living example of cultural resilience.

🌎 Bayterek Tower: Innovation Revitalizing Tradition

Astana’s Bayterek Tower (designed by architect Norman Foster with concept by President Nazarbayev) represents the hybrid model perfectly. It is inspired by the Kazakh mythological World Tree (Baiterek) — an ancient symbol from the Tengriist cosmogony — expressed through 21st-century glass-and-steel architecture. The tower is simultaneously a modern landmark and a carrier of ancient symbolic meaning: the golden egg at the top represents the sun and the mythical firebird Samruk. Ancient meme encoded in modern form.

🇰🇿 Digital Kazakhstan: Modernization Meets Heritage

The state program Digital Kazakhstan (launched 2017) seeks to digitize historical archives, oral epics, ethnographic records, and museum collections. This is a direct application of the Lecture 10 principle: innovation does not threaten tradition but can serve as its carrier. Digitizing the Diwan-i Hikmet, the Kazakh epic corpus, and photographic archives of the steppe makes cultural heritage accessible to the global Kazakh diaspora — and to future generations who may never live on the steppe.

  3. Associative Landscapes: Significant primarily through cultural, historical, or spiritual associations rather than physical modification. Ulytau in central Kazakhstan is such a site: its mountains and rivers carry the memory of khan coronations, tribal assemblies, and the founding moments of the Kazakh state. The land is a repository of collective memory.
Cultural Resilience — Why It Goes Beyond Artifacts (Model Answer)

Cultural resilience is the capacity of a culture to adapt, maintain its core identity, and transmit values and practices across generations even under disruption. The course argues that resilience goes beyond protecting physical artifacts and sites for three reasons:

  1. Knowledge systems matter: Traditional ecological knowledge (e.g., sustainable Kazakh pastoralist practices for managing pasture, water, and herds) cannot be preserved in a museum — it must be lived and practiced. Once a knowledge system dies, no artifact can recover it.
  2. Rituals and living practice: Nauryz is not culturally alive because there are books about it — it is alive because millions of Kazakhs continue to cook nauryz kozhe, clean their homes, and gather with community. Living practice is the carrier.
  3. Stories and identity narratives: The oral epics (Koblandy Batyr, Qozy Körpesh) are not merely literary texts but identity-forming narratives that answer the question “who are we?” These stories must be told, not merely archived.

The course’s concluding quote captures this perfectly: “Cultural resilience is not just about protecting sites or artifacts — it’s about protecting the system of knowledge, the rituals and landscape, and the stories that define who we are.”

Lectures 1–2: Theory Foundations

What is the Latin root of “culture” and what does it mean?

Colere = to cultivate, to inhabit, to honor. First metaphorical use: Cicero’s cultura animi (cultivation of the soul), 1st century BCE.

What are the three elements of culture according to Kroeber & Kluckhohn?

Values (ideas about what is important), Norms (expectations of behavior), Artifacts (material objects derived from values and norms).

What is the difference between “langue” and “parole” in Saussure?

Langue = the abstract language system shared by a community (rules, grammar, vocabulary as a system). Parole = the individual’s actual use of language in specific speech acts. Saussure studied langue, not parole.

What is “unlimited semiosis” (Peirce)?

Each interpretant created by a sign becomes a new sign requiring its own interpretant — creating a potentially infinite chain of interpretation. Meaning is never final but always in process.

How does Barthes define “myth” in cultural semiotics?

A second-order sign system: an existing sign (signifier + signified) becomes the signifier for a new, ideological meaning. Myth naturalizes ideology — making historically constructed meanings appear natural, universal, and inevitable.

Lectures 3–5: Kazakh Historical Culture

What is the Tengriist hierarchy of deities?

1. Buddh Tengri (supreme sky deity, prosperity, state formation). 2. Jol Tengri (God of the Way, freedom, daring). 3. Viruth Tengri (guide of warrior souls to the sky). 4. Erlik (master of the underworld).

What does Diwan Lughat al-Turk mean and why is it important?

“Collection/Dictionary of Turkic Languages.” Written by Mahmud al-Kashgari (11th c.). The only comprehensive source on medieval Turkic material culture, language, folklore, geography, and daily life across 25+ Turkic dialects.

What is a “tolğau”?

The main genre of Kazakh jyrau poetry: a reflective, instructive song about life, death, ethics, and political wisdom. Addressed to khans and community leaders. Combines philosophical reflection with social and political commentary.

Who founded the Kazakh Khanate and when?

Founded in 1465 by Janiybek Khan and Kerei Khan, after their separation from the Abulkhayr Khanate. The first independent Kazakh state.

Lectures 6–10: Modern Kazakhstan

What is “socialist realism”?

The official Soviet artistic doctrine: art must depict reality in its “revolutionary development,” glorifying Soviet achievements and serving Communist Party goals. Kazakh artists worked within (and sometimes against) this framework during the Soviet period.

What are the two critical tensions in global cultural development?

1. Localization of national-state culture (how nations survive as distinct entities and preserve their uniqueness). 2. Integration of various cultures into a single planetary culture (participating in global processes). Kazakhstan must navigate both.

How does Kazakhstan score on Hofstede’s six cultural dimensions?

High Power Distance, Collectivist (not individualist), approximately 50/50 Masculine/Feminine, High Uncertainty Avoidance, Long-Term Orientation, Restrained (not indulgent).

What is the difference between “Kazakhstani” and “Kazakh” identity in cultural policy?

Kazakh identity = ethnic identity (Kazakh ethnicity specifically). Kazakhstani identity = civic identity (all citizens of Kazakhstan regardless of ethnicity). State policy promotes Kazakhstani identity to reflect the multi-ethnic population and avoid ethnic exclusion.

What is “cultural resilience” according to the course conclusion?

“Not just about protecting sites or artifacts — it’s about protecting the system of knowledge, the rituals and landscape, and the stories that define who we are.” Culture must be living and practiced, not merely archived.
